The Canadian Lesbian & Gay Archive, also known as the CLGA, is a nonprofit organization dedicated to preserving the history of the LGBTQ+ community. In fact, they are the biggest LGBTQ+ archive in Canada!

The old UI

Research

The LGBTQ+ community has a long and rich history, and the sad fact is, that is not as well known as it deserves to be. As I conducted user interviews, I learned that this is not only true, but is a problem, among community member and allies. It all started with creating a proto-persona based on research on demographics and possible user problems. Then, 5 user interviews were conducted, all of whom identified as either part of the LGBTQ+ community or an ally. After creating an affinity diagram, it was found that resources such as the CLGA were not well known. 3/5 users were familiar with LGBTQ+ nonprofits on a global scale, and none were familiar with their local organizations, including the CLGA. However, there was an overall desire among users to learn about the LGBTQ+ community’s history.

Key Insight

“I know so little about LGBTQ+ history besides the super obvious stuff, like getting legalized in Canada and the US, and the stonewall riots. Most of what I know about our culture is social stigmas, which scare me into the closet. I have a lot of admiration for openly out LGBTQ+ folks.”

Why does this matter? What is the ideal solution? Then, will that solution have an impact? And what is that impact?

...These were some of the questions I was left with after my research.

While the demographic of the userbase was already assumed, the target user was still undefined. After consulting the research, and empathy map was created and the target user as a person interested in LGBTQ+ history who would make use of their services and facilities.

The Problem:

The CLGA’s current website does not reflect it as an organization, and lacks branding.

If the website is redesigned and given distinct, memorable branding, it would be easier to use, and therefore it will be easier to make impressions on users who may be more likely to support or get involved with the organization.

Due to time constraints, a paper prototype was not made However, I made the equivalent within a digital wireframe in Figma. I referred to my user flow to turn it into my clickable low-fidelity prototype.
Then, I conducted usability testing to learn what I can improve upon when I make my high-fidelity prototype.

For every round of usability testing, users were to navigate to the Book A Tour page then follow the steps to book a tour for a specific date and time.

The following feedback was received and iterated upon:

  • Timeslot selection is unclear, confuses the user & disrupts the user flow
  • Colour scheme scheme was too distracting, especially the button’s hover state
  • Buttons were a bit large
  • The calendar needed a clearer indication of being selected/active

Then, A/B testing was conducted to determine the effectiveness of the prototype’s colour palette.
